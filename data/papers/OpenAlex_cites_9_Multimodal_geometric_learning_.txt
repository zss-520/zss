【文献标题】: Multimodal geometric learning for antimicrobial peptide identification by leveraging alphafold2-predicted structures and surface features
【发表年份】: 2025
【引用次数】: 9
【PDF 直链】: https://doi.org/10.1093/bib/bbaf261

【摘要】: Antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) are short peptides that play critical roles in diverse biological processes and exhibit functional activities against target organisms. While numerous methods have demonstrated the effectiveness of deep neural networks for AMP identification using sequence features; nevertheless, higher-level peptide characteristics-such as 3D structure and geometric surface features-have not been comprehensively explored. To address this gap, we introduce the SSFGM-Model (Sequence, Structure, Surface, Graph, and Geometric-based Model), a novel framework that integrates multiple feature types to enhance AMP identification. The model represents each peptide sequence as a graph, where nodes are characterized by amino acid features derived from ProteinBERT, ESM-2, and One-hot embeddings. Graph convolutional networks and an attention mechanism are employed to capture high-order structural and sequential relationships. Additionally, surface geometry and physicochemical properties are processed using a geometric neural network. Finally, a feature fusion strategy combines the outputs from these subnetworks to enable robust AMP identification. Extensive benchmarking experiments demonstrate that the SSFGM-Model outperforms current state-of-the-art methods. An ablation study further confirms the critical role of sequence, structural, and surface features in AMP identification. The key contribution of this work is the innovative integration of multiple levels of peptide characteristics and the combination of geometric and graph neural networks. This approach provides a more comprehensive understanding of the sequence-structure-function relationship of peptides, paving the way for more accurate AMP prediction. The SSFGM-Model has a significant potential for applications in the discovery and design of novel AMP-based therapeutics. The source code is publicly available at https://github.com/ggcameronnogg/SSFGM-Model.

Keywords: antimicrobial peptides; sequence features; surface features; structure information; geometric learning; AlphaFold2; graph neural network Introduction In recent years, the excessive and improper use of antibiotics has led to increasing antibiotic resistance of pathogenic bac- teria. The development of antibiotic-resistance genes and mul- tidrug resistance during bacterial evolution can greatly reducethe effectiveness of antibiotic treatments [ 1]. The escalation in the number of antibiotic-resistant bacterial isolates, accompanied by the bottlenecks in antibiotic discovery, has resulted in theresurgence of easy-treated diseases [ 2]. Consequently, antibiotic- resistant bacteria have emerged as a growing global health threat [3]. Meanwhile, antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) have emerged as a promising alternative to effectively combat antibiotic-resistant bacteria using unique antimicrobial mechanisms [ 4]. AMPs are aclass of natural or synthetic peptide molecules. They are com- posed of short amino acid sequences, typically ranging from 10 to 50 amino acids. These peptides are widely distributed innature. AMPs represent a crucial defense mechanism that organ- isms have developed through evolution [ 5]. Unlike traditional antibiotics that target specific sites, AMPs exhibit broad-spectrumactivity by disrupting pathogen membranes or intracellular com- ponents, leading to cell death. Their versatility allows them to be integrated with advanced technologies, such as nanoparticle-based delivery systems, for clinical applications [ 6]. This signif- icantly reduces the risk of emerging drug-resistant bacteria [ 7]. These highly diverse peptides, which leverage surface character-istics like polarity, disrupt the membranes of other organisms,Downloaded from https://academic.oup.com/bib/article/26/3/bbaf261/8156471 by guest on 18 April 2026 2|Sun et al. ultimately inducing cell death [ 8]. Despite their high-antibacterial activity and functional diversity, the experimental discovery of AMPs is still hindered by efficiency and cost limitations. Therefore, an efficient computational platform that can accurately identifythe antimicrobial properties of AMPs might accelerate the clinical application of AMP-based therapeutics [ 9]. The rapid growth of peptide datasets has spurred the creation of numerous peptide data platforms, such as the Peptide Util- ity search server, providing researchers with rich resources for AMP discovery [ 10]. Concurrently, deep learning models, com- bined with extensive datasets, have emerged as powerful compu- tational tools in biological science, providing substantial savingsin time and resources [ 11]. In recent years, AI-based approaches have been extensively applied to AMP identification [ 12]. For instance, tools like AMP Scanner [ 13], AMPfun [ 14], and Deep- AmPEP30 [ 15] utilize a diverse machine learning and deep learn- ing techniques, including classification and regression trees [ 16], convolutional neural networks (CNN) [ 17], and long short-term memory networks [ 18], to analyze AMP sequences and predict their antibacterial activities. These approaches primarily focus on manipulating sequence-based features for AMP classification andfunctional prediction. However, AMPs are structurally diverse polymers, categorized by their secondary structures into α-helical, β-sheet, extend- ed/random coil, and loop (cyclic) types [ 19]. Sequence-based rep- resentations are often insufficient for comprehensive analysis [20]. To address this limitation, graph neural networks (GNNs) appear as a robust approach for the structural modeling of AMPs [21]. GNN effectively incorporates spatial connections and adja- cent information between atoms or amino acid nodes, enabling amore holistic representation of peptides. It also allows for a com- prehensive understanding of how structural variations influence the peptide’s antimicrobial activity [ 22]. For instance, sAMPpred- GAT [ 23] and esm-AxP-GDL [ 24] leveraged predicted peptide struc- tures alongside GNN-based model to distinguish AMPs from non- AMPs. sAMPpred-GAT utilized trRosetta [ 25] for structure predic- tion, and then incorporated both structure and sequence infor- mation to enhance the attention-based learning for AMP classifi- cation [ 23]. Similarly, esm-AxP-GDL combined ESMFold-predicted structures with intermediate protein language model features extracted by ESM-2 [ 26] in the form of a graph-based model [ 24]. AMPs typically exert their antimicrobial effects by directly interacting with targets like bacterial membranes or proteins. Electrostatic attraction and hydrophobic interactions disruptmembranes or interfere with intracellular processes, ultimately causing bacterial cell death [ 12]. However, existing predictors did not consider the biophysical constraint and only modeled theamino acid level graph representing the whole AMP structure. We hypothesized that focusing on the functional surfaces of AMPs, particularly regions involved in protein-peptide interactions,could enhance predictions of AMP activity. Molecular Surface Interaction Fingerprinting (MaSIF) [ 20] provided a protocol to capture protein surfaces and compute a series of geometricand physiochemical features, including shape index, distance- dependent curvature, hydropathy, Poisson–Boltzmann continuum electrostatics, and free electrons/protons [ 20]. These features were extracted from AlphaFold2-predicted peptide structures, thereby supporting a more comprehensive analysis of AMP’s functional properties. In this study, we develop a multimodal geometric deep learning framework termed SSFGM-Model. It integrates the AlphaFold2- predicted structure with the pretrained ESM-2 and ProteinBERTrepresentations into a GNN-based architecture, further enhanced by peptide surface fingerprints, to identify functional AMPs.We collect an extensive dataset of AMP sequences and structural data, providing a basis for representing the complex charac- teristics of these peptides. Furthermore, we perform extensive benchmarking experiments and ablation studies to illustrate theadvantages of incorporating structural and surface inputs. As a result, SSFGM-Model demonstrates superior performance in predicting AMPs compared to the existing prediction tools. It mayhave practical implications for the discovery of novel AMPs and the development of future antimicrobial strategies. Material and methods Data collection and pre-processing Benchmark dataset 1 The Benchmark Dataset 1 consists of 8222 peptide sequences from three subsets: (i) 2021 AMPs and 2021 non-AMPs retrieved from the AMP Scanner vr.2 web server ( www. ampscanner. com ), which included the updated sequences from the APD3 [ 27] database; (ii) 1100 unique AMP sequences derived from the original APD3 database [ 27]; (iii) 1536 AMPs and 1536 non-AMPs sourced from the reference (Xu et al. ). The sequences in the third subset were aggregated from various resources, including ADAM [ 28], ADAPTABLE [ 29], APD3 [ 27], CAMP [ 30], dbAMP [ 31], DRAMP [ 32,33], LAMP [ 34,35], MilkAMP [ 36], and YADAMP [ 37]. We randomly shuffled the data from the three subsets and split the dataset into training (2878 AMPs and 2600 non-AMPs), validation (895 AMPs and 656 non-AMPs), and test sets (894 AMPs and 301non-AMPs). The length distribution of the peptide sequences is illustrated in Fig. 1b (top). AMPs are generally shorter, primarily ranging from 25 to 50 amino acids, with a consistent distributionacross the training, evaluation, and test sets. In contrast, non- AMPs exhibit a wider range of sequence lengths, spanning from 25 to nearly 150 amino acids. The test set was designed to assess themodel’s generalizability under the imbalanced data distribution. Benchmark dataset 2 The Benchmark Dataset 2 was also derived from the AMP Scan- ner web server [ 13].Unlike the updated version in Benchmark Dataset 1, it was frequently used for model training in recentdeep-learning research on AMPs. Many computational tools have benchmarked their performance on this dataset. It consists of 1778 AMP and 1778 non-AMP sequences in which AMPs were primarily sourced from the APD3 database [ 27], targeting Gram- positive or Gram-negative bacteria. To ensure data quality, weconfirmed that the dataset was curated by excluding sequences shorter than 10 amino acid residues and using the CD-HIT pro- gram to remove sequences with ≥90% sequence identity. We selected 2132 sequences (1066 AMPs and 1066 non-AMPs) for training and 1424 sequences (712 AMPs and 712 non-AMPs) for testing, as shown in Fig. 1a (right), which was consistent with the data splitting setting of the existing predictors. The non-AMPs were obtained from the UniProt [ 38] database excluding peptides with possible antimicrobial activities in the same manner asBenchmark Dataset 1. Additionally, non-AMPs shorter than 10 amino acid residues or sharing ≥40% sequence identity were removed. The length distribution patterns of AMPs and non-AMPsin Benchmark Dataset 2 were similar to those in Benchmark Dataset 1, as illustrated in Fig. 1b (bottom). It is important to note that while some peptide sequences overlap between BenchmarkDataset 1 and Benchmark Dataset 2, the training, validation, and test sets within each dataset are entirely independent. This ensures no overlap between the training and test sets, allowingfor a fair and unbiased evaluation of the model’s performance [ 13]. Detailed information about Bench- mark Dataset 3 is provided in Supplementary Material Text S4 . The overview of SSFGM-model The framework of SSFGM-Model is shown in Fig. 2 . The input peptide sequences were embedded as pretrained feature vectorsusing two advanced protein language models, ProteinBert [ 40] and ESM-2 [ 26], which represent evolutionary information, along with One-hot encodings. AlphaFold2 [ 41] was employed to predict the 3D structures from peptide sequences. To construct the peptide graph, amino acid residues were selected as nodes and the residue-level embeddings were used as the correspondingnode features. Structural relationships between amino acid residues were captured based on the pairwise Euclidean distance between C αatoms. Specifically, when the distance between two residue nodes was below a specific threshold (e.g. 8 Å and 10 Å in this study), an edge was established between them. The distance between residues could also serve as the edge attribute.A GNN-based network consisting of six graph convolutional network (GCN) layers and one graph attention network (GAT) layer was further utilized to perform feature propagation on thepeptide graph. On the other hand, we referred to MaSIF [ 20]t o extract geometric features (shape index and distance-dependent curvature) and chemical features (hydropathy index, continuumelectrostatics, and locations of free electrons and proton donors) [ 20] from the peptide surface patches. These features were mapped onto the geodesic polar coordinates to describe therelative positions within the local surface patch. The geometric neural network was subsequently applied to the learning the underlying biophysical representations. Finally, the model fused the outputs of the two subnetworks to enhance the prediction forantimicrobial activity. In addition to the SSFGM-Model, we developed a sequence- feature-based model, SEQC-Model (Sequence-based CNN Model),which integrated sequence information using a two-layer CNN. T wo structure-based models were also established in this study: (i) SSGT-Model (Sequence and Structure-based GCN-TransformerModel) combining three GCN layers, two transformer layers with eight heads, and two GAT layers; (ii) SSGA-Model (Sequence and Structure-based GCN with Attention Model) employing six GCNlayers and a single GAT layer. These models were all evaluated to assess the contribution of the key components in the SSFGM model. Features selection Sequence-level embedding One-hot encoding One-hot encoding was employed in this study to represent input amino acid residues. Specifically, each amino acid residue is encoded as a binary vector of fixed length, where only oneposition corresponding to the specific residue is set to 1, while all other positions are set to 0. Consequently, an entire amino acid sequence can be encoded as a two-dimensional matrix ofsize [ L,NOnehot ]w h e r e Ldenotes the length of amino acid sequence and NOnehot is 20, indicating the number of standard amino acids. It is capable of handling long- range dependencies within protein sequences. ESM-2 embedding The ESM-2 model utilizes self-supervised learning on large-scale protein sequence databases, primarily the UniRef [ 42] database and MGnify [ 43]. Protein sequences are processed through multi- ple layers of Transformer encoders, which employ self-attention mechanisms and feed-forward neural networks [ 44]. The model can generate latent feature representations for each amino acid residue [ 45], capturing both syntactic and semantic information within the sequence, as well as interactions between residuesand three-dimensional structural information [ 26]. In this study, we employed ESM-2 3B model to embed the peptide sequences, representing each sequence as a two-dimensional matrix of size[ L,NESM2]w h e r e Ldenotes the length of amino acid sequence and NESM2is 2560. Structure-level embedding and graph construction We employed the ColabFold [ 46] version of AlphaFold2 [ 41]t o predict the three-dimensional structures of all peptide sequencesin our dataset, generating corresponding Protein Data Bank (PDB) files containing atomic coordinates of the peptides, where a PDB file is a standard text format used to represent thethree-dimensional structure of biological macromolecules [ 47]. Graphs were constructed based on these coordinates where the edges represent potential interactions between amino acidresidues, such as hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, and van der Waals forces [ 48]. The three-dimensional coordinates provide information about the distances betweenamino acid residues, reflecting their spatial interactions [ 49].Thus, we extracted the three-dimensional coordinates of each peptide sequence from the predicted structure with the highestpredicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) score [ 41]. The positions of the C αatoms in all standard amino acids were selected to represent the residue-level coordinates. Next, edgeswere constructed in the graph based on the Euclidean distances between residues. By calculating the distances and applying a threshold (10 Å or 8 Å in this study), we determined whether anundirected edge should be established between two amino acid residues. The corresponding distance was also included as an edge attribute. This approach enabled the graph to reflect spatialproximity between residues, helping the GNN-based network capture structural interactions within the peptide. With regard to the node features, we combined the sequence- level feature vectors, including One-hot encoding, ProteinBERT embeddings, and ESM-2 embeddings. Each node represented an amino acid residue, with its sequence features, while the edgescaptured the spatial interactions between residues. Using this approach, we generated peptide graphs that incorporate both sequence and structural information, which were used to train the graph neural network for AMP classification. Surface-level features The geometric and chemical features for each vertex on the protein surface were extracted from the three-dimensional struc-tures of peptide sequences predicted by AlphaFold2. Each peptide covered several surface patches with a geodesic radius of r=12Å and 100 vertices [ 20], And each vertex was characterized by five geometric or physiochemical properties: shape index, distance- dependent curvature, Poisson–Boltzmann continuum electrostat- ics, free electrons and proton donors, and hydropathy [ 20]. Shape index The shape index represents the local geometric shape of eachvertex, indicating the degree of concavity or convexity [ 50]. Distance-dependent curvature Distance-dependent curvature represents the relationship between the distance from a vertex to the patch center and the surface normal at those points [ 50]. This relationship is expressed as a curvature value, reflecting the relative geometric shapebetween the vertex and the center. When the curvature value approaches −0.7, the region around the vertex is concave relative to the patch center; when the value approaches +0.7, the vertex is more convex compared to the patch center [ 20]. Free electrons and proton donors Free electrons and proton donors describe the potential of aprotein surface vertex to act as a hydrogen bond donor or acceptor. Calculating the likelihood of each vertex being a free electron(hydrogen bond acceptor) or proton donor (hydrogen bond donor) helps the model identify regions on the protein surface that may participate in intermolecular interactions. Values closer to −1 indicate an optimal hydrogen bond acceptor site, while values closer to +1 indicate an optimal hydrogen bond donor site [ 20].
