【摘要】: Bioactive peptide therapeutics has been a long-standing research topic. Notably, the antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) have been extensively studied for its therapeutic potential. Meanwhile, the demand for annotating other therapeutic peptides, such as antiviral peptides (AVPs) and anticancer peptides (ACPs), also witnessed an increase in recent years. However, we conceive that the structure of peptide chains and the intrinsic information between the amino acids is not fully investigated among the existing protocols. Therefore, we develop a new graph deep learning model, namely TP-LMMSG, which offers lightweight and easy-to-deploy advantages while improving the annotation performance in a generalizable manner. The results indicate that our model can accurately predict the properties of different peptides. The model surpasses the other state-of-the-art models on AMP, AVP and ACP prediction across multiple experimental validated datasets. Moreover, TP-LMMSG also addresses the challenges of time-consuming pre-processing in graph neural network frameworks. With its flexibility in integrating heterogeneous peptide features, our model can provide substantial impacts on the screening and discovery of therapeutic peptides. The source code is available at https://github.com/NanjunChen37/TP_LMMSG.

Introduction The discovery of peptide therapeutics has always been a focal point of drug development. Distinguished from small molecule drugs and biological agents, peptide drugs capture researchers’ attention by their unique advantages [ 1, 2]. In recent years, the progress in synthesis, modification, and analytical technologies have propelled therapeutic peptide research into a new era [ 2]. Concurrently, the continuous evolution of machine learning models has played a pivotal role in accelerating the peptide drug discovery pipeline. Alongside the widely acknowledged antimicrobial peptides (AMPs) prediction models, the field has witnessed the successive emergence of antiviral peptides (AVPs) and anticancer peptides (ACPs) prediction models [ 3–5]. The AMPs have attracted widespread attention due to their broad-spectrum antibacterial activity [ 6, 7]. Specifically, AMPs belongs to a class of short peptides (around 6 to 100 amino acids) involved in the innate immune response among lives [8, 9]. The general therapeutic mechanism of AMPs is to bind the surface of microorganisms through the attraction effect between positive and negative charges. Specifically, most AMPs have net-positive charge, while bacterial membrane has negative charge. The interaction can directly or indirectly destroy the integrity of microbial membrane, thereby affecting the life cycle and proliferation of the microorganism [ 10, 11]. Moreover, evidence also suggests that the 3D structure of AMPs may also affect the antimicrobial activity of peptides [ 12,13].In addition to natural AMPs,it has been proven promising to discover new synthetic AMPs using generative models and synthetic workflows [ 14, 15]. Different from small molecules,Downloaded from https://academic.oup.com/bib/article/25/4/bbae308/7699353 by guest on 18 April 2026 2|Chenet al. peptide therapies mainly take advantage of higher specificity and lower toxicity. Hence, peptide drugs with specific targets are also adapted as antifungal, antiviral, antiparasitic or even anticancer clinical strategies [ 8, 16, 17]. To meet the increasing demand for model training and high-thr oughput screening of bioactive peptides, various public databases have been established. These databases provide different types of peptide information, allowing researchers to design machine learning models based on different objectives. The APD3 [ 9], dbAMP [ 18]a n d D R A M P[ 19] databases collect available AMP data and classify those AMPs into different categories based on their targets. Moreover, the experimental data and derived data, such as inhibition activity and molecular properties, are also included in these databases. Meanwhile, the databases such as CAMP [ 20], ADAM [ 21] and SATPdb [ 22] provide abundant peptide structural information.In 2021,several experimentally validated datasets were investigated [ 3],including APD3 [9], CAMP [ 20], dbAMP [ 18], DRAMP [ 19], LAMP [ 23]a n d YADAMP [ 24]. Based on the AMPs records in these datasets, a fusion dataset named XUAMP is constructed to thoroughly evaluate the performance of AMP identification models [ 3]. Meanwhile , the CD-HIT tool is utilized for rigorous sequence filtering and sampling to construct the independent test dataset [ 3]. The following model evaluations demonstrate that there is a potential for further enhancement in current AMP prediction models. In addition to AMPs, the APD3, CAMP, DRAMP and LAMP databases also contain diverse peptide information, including AVPs, ACPs and other theraputic peptides that target at different diseases. Benefit from the peptide information in the aforementioned datasets , multiple feature representation approaches have also been proposed. Besides the commonly used one-hot encoding and multiple sequence alignment (MSA) encoding, incorporating amino acid physicochemical properties can also effectively improve model performance [ 25, 26]. Meanwhile, the use of positional encoding, amino acid composition and pseudo amino acid composition features was shown to enhance the model performance by incorporating detailed composition information of amino acids [ 25, 27, 28]. As for the prediction models, the random forest model amPEPpy demonstrates good predictive performance in distinguishing AMPs [ 29]. Recently, there has been an increasing trend in utilizing deep learning frameworks to distinguish AMPs and non-AMPs [ 30, 31]. Besides the com- position features, the recently published AMPpred-MFA model incorporates dipeptide and k-mer composition as additional input features. Although these methods combine the position, composition and physicochemical properties of peptides, they do not leverage the structural information of the peptide. To tackle this, the sAMPpred-GAT model has been proposed. The model utilizes the protein structure predicted by trRosetta for AMP identification [ 32, 33]. Except for AMP prediction, some works have transferred the successful experience of predicting AMPs to other types of peptide identification tasks, and have developed prediction models specifically for AVP and ACP [ 4, 5]. Although sAMPpred-GAT is a promising and innovative attempt, there are still limitations in its practical application. First, the model only utilizes one-hot encoding, positional encoding, hidden Markov models (HMMs) encoding and position-specific scoring matrix (PSSM) encoding as the node features.Two of these features (HMM and PSSM) solely rely on MSA and protein sequence databases [ 34, 35].This indicates that the model lacks explicit representations of amino acid physicochemical properties. The two rounds of MSA also implies that the bottleneck in the model’s runtime lies in the feature extraction stage (e.g. around 15 s on single sample). Additionally, its complete deployment requires the download of theentirenonredundantproteinsequencedatabase(nrdb,0.5TB). Therefore, it is crucial to improve the effectiveness and storage occupancy of the model. In this study, we aim to develop a peptide prediction model, whichnotonlypredictsAMPs,butalsogeneralizestopredictother types of peptides (i.e. AVPs and ACPs). Moreover, we aim to reduce the storage occupancy and feature encoding time in graph neu- ral network applications. So far, protein embeddings have been demonstrated to be successful at both sequence and amino acid levels [36, 37]. Meanwhile, the hierarchical multi-scale residual network (HMSRN) has been proved promising in protein–RNA interactionpredictiontasks[ 38].Therefore,weadaptmultiplepre- trained language models to obtain the amino acid embeddings. The HMSRN is then applied to extract informative features among the language model embeddings. Subsequently, we concatenate the extracted features into each node of peptide graph for graph learning. We name our approach as the Therapeutic Peptide Language Model Multi-Scale Graph neural network (TP-LMMSG). The experimental results reveal that our model outperforms the state-of-the-art (SOTA) models in multiple evaluation metrics. Remarkably, these achievements are attained without employing HMM features and rely solely on the lightweight nonredundant protein sequence database 90 (nrdb90) [ 39]. In addition to AMP prediction, we further generalize our model to the prediction tasks of AVPs and ACPs. Our model also exhibits remarkable performance, surpassing existing models in identifying the other types of peptides with imbalanced datasets. Material and methods Datasets To ensure a fair evaluation and enable an objective compar-ison with previously proposed models, we primarily utilize the seven AMP datasets provided in previous literatures (i.e. XUAMP, APD3, CAMP, dbAMP, DRAMP, LAMP and YADAMP [ 3, 9, 18–20, 23, 24, 32]). Specifically, the XUAMP dataset is integrated from multiple datasets including APD3, DRAMP, LAMP, CAMP, dbAMP and YADAMP, resulting in 5536 AMPs/nonAMPs in the training set, and 1536 AMPs/nonAMPs in the independent test set. We choose to benchmark on this dataset as the primary evaluation. Besides the XUAMP dataset, we also conduct evaluations on the other six datasets mentioned above ( Table 1, Figure S1 ). Specifically, each independent test dataset of the six datasets has undergone strict filtering rules,where CD-HIT tool is applied to ensure no sequence shares more than 40% identity in the test set [ 3, 40, 41]. In this manner, we can rigorously evaluate the performance and make a thorough comparison with previously proposed methods. As for the AVPs and ACPs datasets, the number of positive samples is considerably fewer than that for AMPs [ 17]. To ensure a fair comparison, we adapt the dataset utilized in previous publications, which are FFMAP AVPs dataset and ACP-ML ACPs dataset, respectively ( Table 1)[4, 5]. Protein language model based embeddings extraction In our lightweight work frame where HMM feature is absent, the sparse node feature representation becomes even scarcer. Furthermore, the shrinkage on nrdb for PSSM encoding poses challenges in accurately representing amino acid features.There-fore, we utilized multiple pre-trained protein language models to obtain informative amino acid feature embeddings ( Figure 1C ) [36, 37]. First, the pretrained SeqVec model (‘sv’ in the following description) is adapted [ 36]. By training EMLo on the UniRef50 dataset, SeqVec can generate continuous embeddings at amino acid level. In this step, the peptide sequences are fed into the pre-trained SeqVec model to obtain amino acid-level embeddings for each sequence. As a result, the model outputs a vector with 1024 dimensions for each amino acid. Due to the varying lengths of sequences, we extract embeddings according to the maximum sequence length ( Lmax). For sequences shorter than Lmax, padding operation was applied at the end of the sequence. As a result, the embeddings matrix for each sequence is Xlm ∈ RLmax×1024,w h e r e lm =sv. WealsoutilizethePortTransmodel,aseriesofproteinlanguage models pre-trained based on BERT, Albert, Electra and T5 [ 37]. We select three lightweight pre-trained models as the methods for amino acid-level feature extraction, which are 1. ProtT5-XL-U50, 2. ProtT5-XL-BFD and 3. ProtBert-BFD. The models will be labeled as ‘uni’, ‘bfd’ and ‘brt’ in the following description. To enable the downstream model to accept the output embeddings from a flexible number of language models,the output embeddings of each language model is stored separately.
